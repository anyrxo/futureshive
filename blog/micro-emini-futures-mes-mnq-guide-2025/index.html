What Are Micro E-mini Futures?

Micro E-mini futures are exchange-traded futures contracts that are 1/10th the size of standard E-mini futures. They were introduced by CME Group on May 6, 2019, to make futures trading accessible to retail traders and smaller accounts.

üìå Key Concept: If standard ES futures have a $50 multiplier (each point = $50), then MES has a $5 multiplier (each point = $5). This means you need approximately 10x less capital to trade the same underlying index.

Why Trade Micro E-mini Futures?

1. Lower Capital Requirements

The #1 reason traders choose micro E-minis is accessibility. You can start trading with as little as $500-$1,000 in your account, compared to $5,000-$25,000 needed for standard E-minis with proper risk management.

Real Example: A trader with $1,000 can comfortably trade 1-2 MES contracts, risking 1-2% per trade ($10-20). The same trader would be overleveraged trying to trade ES, which requires $500+ margin and has $12.50 tick value.

2. Better Risk Management

Smaller contract size means more precise position sizing. Instead of being forced to trade a full ES contract, you can scale into positions gradually:

  • Entry 1: 2 MES contracts at key level
  • Entry 2: Add 2 more MES on confirmation
  • Entry 3: Final 2 MES on breakout

With ES, you'd have to risk a full $12.50/tick from the start or wait for all confirmations, potentially missing the move.

3. Perfect for Learning and Testing Strategies

Paper trading doesn't replicate real emotions. Micro E-minis let you trade real market conditions with real money but at a fraction of the cost. Test strategies, build discipline, and gain experience without risking thousands per trade.

4. Same Market, Same Opportunities

Micro E-minis track the exact same indices as standard contracts. You get:

  • Same price movements and correlations
  • Same trading hours (23.5 hours/day)
  • Same market depth and order flow patterns
  • Same technical analysis and indicators work

5. Lower Commission Impact

Commissions on micro contracts are typically $0.50-$1.00 per side vs $1.24-$4.00 for standard E-minis. As a percentage of tick value, this is more favorable for smaller accounts.

üìä Example Calculation:
MES Commission Impact: $0.60 commission on $1.25 tick = 48% of one tick
ES Commission Impact: $1.24 commission on $12.50 tick = 10% of one tick

While ES has better percentage cost, MES's absolute dollar cost is lower, making it better for frequent traders with smaller accounts.

Step 4: Develop a Trading Strategy

Micro E-minis respond to the same strategies as standard contracts. Popular approaches:

Opening Range Breakout (ORB)

Trade the breakout of the first 30-60 minutes after market open (9:30 AM ET). MES/MNQ often show clear directional moves after the opening range is established.

VWAP Strategy

Use Volume Weighted Average Price as dynamic support/resistance. Buy dips to VWAP in uptrends, short rallies to VWAP in downtrends. Works exceptionally well on MES.

Momentum Scalping

Take quick 2-5 point moves using volume profile and order flow. MNQ's volatility makes it ideal for scalping 10-20 point moves (worth $20-40 per contract).

News Trading

Trade economic data releases (CPI, NFP, FOMC). MES/MNQ spike heavily during major news, offering 20-50 point moves in minutes.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Overleveraging Because "It's Micro"

Just because margin is low doesn't mean you should trade 10 contracts with a $1,000 account. Stick to the 1-2% risk rule regardless of contract size.

2. Ignoring Commission Impact

At $0.50-$1.00 per side, a round trip costs $1.00-$2.00. On MES with $1.25 ticks, you need nearly 2 ticks just to break even. Factor commissions into your strategy.

3. Not Accounting for Slippage

While micro E-minis have good liquidity, they're still less liquid than ES/NQ. During fast markets, you may experience 1-2 ticks of slippage. Build this into your risk calculations.

4. Trading Overnight Without Experience

Micro E-minis trade 23.5 hours, but overnight sessions (6 PM - 9:30 AM ET) have lower volume and wider spreads. Master daytime trading first before attempting overnight sessions.

5. Switching to Standard E-minis Too Quickly

Don't rush to trade ES/NQ because you had a few winning trades on micros. Prove consistent profitability over 3-6 months with micro contracts before scaling up.

Tax Considerations for Micro E-mini Futures

Futures traders in the US benefit from 60/40 tax treatment under IRS Section 1256:

  • 60% of gains/losses are taxed as long-term capital gains (lower rate)
  • 40% of gains/losses are taxed as short-term capital gains
  • Mark-to-market accounting applies (all positions marked at year-end)

This favorable tax treatment applies to micro E-minis just like standard contracts. Consult a tax professional familiar with futures trading for specific guidance.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between E-mini and Micro E-mini futures?
Micro E-mini futures are 1/10th the size of standard E-mini contracts. For example, MES has a $5 multiplier ($1.25/tick) vs ES with $50 multiplier ($12.50/tick). MNQ has a $2 multiplier ($0.50/tick) vs NQ with $20 multiplier ($5/tick). This means micro contracts require approximately 10x less capital and margin, making them perfect for beginners or traders with smaller accounts.
How much money do I need to trade Micro E-mini futures?
As of 2025, you need approximately $50-$100 for MES (Micro E-mini S&P 500) and $100-$300 for MNQ (Micro E-mini Nasdaq-100) in margin per contract, depending on your broker. However, professional traders recommend having at least $500-$1,000 total account balance for proper risk management, allowing you to risk only 1-2% per trade.
Can you make money trading Micro E-mini futures?
Yes, you can make money trading micro E-mini futures, but it requires skill, discipline, and proper risk management. With MES, each point move equals $5 (vs $50 for ES). While the profit potential per contract is smaller, micro futures allow you to start with less capital, practice strategies with real market conditions, and scale up as you prove profitability. Many successful traders started with micro contracts before moving to standard E-minis.
What are the trading hours for Micro E-mini futures?
Micro E-mini futures trade nearly 24 hours a day, 5 days a week on CME Globex. Trading begins Sunday at 6:00 PM ET and runs until Friday at 5:00 PM ET, with a daily maintenance break from 5:00 PM to 6:00 PM ET. The highest volume occurs during Regular Trading Hours (RTH): 9:30 AM to 4:00 PM ET when the stock market is open. This extended trading allows you to trade around news events, overnight gaps, and international market moves.
Which is better for beginners: MES or MNQ?
MES (Micro E-mini S&P 500) is generally better for beginners because it's less volatile and requires lower margin ($50-100 vs $100-300 for MNQ). MES tracks the broader S&P 500 index with 500 companies, providing more stable price action. MNQ tracks the Nasdaq-100 (heavily tech-weighted), offering higher volatility and profit potential but also greater risk. Start with MES to learn futures mechanics, then consider MNQ once you're consistently profitable.
Do prop firms allow trading Micro E-mini futures?
Yes, many prop firms allow micro E-mini futures trading, including Apex Trader Funding, TopStep, My Funded Futures, and Earn2Trade. However, most prop firms provide standard E-mini contracts (ES, NQ) in their funded accounts since they're providing the capital. What is the tick size and value for Micro E-mini futures?
Tick sizes and values vary by contract: MES (Micro S&P 500): 0.25 point tick = $1.25 per tick. MNQ (Micro Nasdaq-100): 0.25 point tick = $0.50 per tick. M2K (Micro Russell 2000): 0.10 point tick = $0.50 per tick. MYM (Micro Dow): 1.00 point tick = $0.50 per tick. These smaller tick values allow for tighter stop losses and more precise position sizing compared to standard E-mini contracts.
Can I trade Micro E-mini futures with $500?
Yes, $500 is a reasonable starting capital for micro E-mini futures, particularly MES. With $500, you can comfortably trade 1-2 MES contracts while following the 1-2% risk rule (risking $5-10 per trade). This allows you to survive multiple losses while learning. For MNQ, which requires higher margin and is more volatile, $500 would limit you to 1 contract with tighter risk management. Most professional traders recommend $1,000+ for more comfortable position sizing and risk management.
